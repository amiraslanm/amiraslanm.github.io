Fire detection and alarm system overview

01

SYSTEM PURPOSE

Why fire detection and alarm systems are designed

A fire alarm system is not just a group of detectors and sounders. It is a coordinated safety system intended to detect fire early, warn occupants, support evacuation and communicate with other fire-safety systems.

LIFE SAFETY

Protection of life

The system provides early warning so occupants can react before escape routes become unsafe due to smoke, fire or toxic gases.

PROPERTY

Protection of property

Early detection can reduce the time between ignition and intervention, supporting firefighting response and reducing damage.

COORDINATION

System integration

Fire alarm design must coordinate with sprinklers, smoke control, access control, elevators, emergency communication and cause-and-effect logic.

SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE

Main parts of a fire alarm system

The basic logic is simple: inputs detect or report a fire condition, the control panel processes the signal, and outputs warn people or command other systems.

INPUTS

Detection and manual alarm

Smoke detectors, heat detectors, beam detectors, duct detectors, manual call points, sprinkler water-flow switches and supervisory inputs are typical alarm inputs.

CONTROL

CIE / fire alarm control panel

The panel identifies zones or device addresses, manages alarm/fault/supervisory states and runs the approved cause-and-effect sequence.

OUTPUTS

Warning and control outputs

Sounders, strobes, bells, speakers, control modules and auxiliary interfaces warn occupants and coordinate other building systems.

03

BS5839-1 CATEGORIES

Design categories at a glance

BS5839-1 uses categories to describe the objective and extent of protection. The category must be agreed with the client, user, insurer, authority or fire strategy team.

M

Manual system using manual call points only; no automatic fire detection.

L1 / L2 / L3 / L4 / L5

Life-safety categories, from full building coverage to defined areas or specific fire-safety objectives.

P1 / P2

Property-protection categories, from full building coverage to defined high-risk or high-value areas.

Practical idea

A fire alarm category is not a decorative label. It changes detector coverage, zoning, warning strategy, documentation and acceptance criteria.

04

DETECTOR SELECTION

Choosing the right detector

No two fires are exactly the same. Detector selection should consider the expected fire signature, room use, smoke, steam, dust, humidity, temperature variation and false-alarm risk.

SMOKE DETECTORS

Optical / ionisation / aspirating

Useful where early smoke detection is important. Avoid using them blindly in spaces with normal smoke, steam or dust.

HEAT DETECTORS

Fixed temperature / rate-of-rise

Often used in kitchens, parking areas or dusty spaces where smoke detectors can cause nuisance alarms; they are generally less suitable as the main life-safety detector.

SPECIAL DETECTORS

Beam, flame, duct and multi-criteria

Large/high rooms, industrial hazards, ducts and challenging environments may need special detectors or multi-criteria detection to balance speed and false-alarm control.

05

DETECTION + ALARM ZONES

Zoning logic

Zoning helps responders quickly identify the fire location and helps designers build a clear evacuation and alarm strategy.

DETECTION ZONES

Where the fire is detected

Detection zones are used to identify fire location. Typical checks include maximum zone area, storey separation, stair/vertical shaft separation and practical search distance.

ALARM ZONES

Where the alarm signal is given

Alarm zones define where evacuation, alert or staged alarm signals operate. In simple buildings, all devices may operate together; complex buildings may need phased evacuation.

MANUAL CALL POINTS

Manual call-point placement

MCPs should support escape routes and storey exits, and their zoning must help occupants and firefighters understand where the alarm originated.

VOID + VERTICAL AREAS

Voids, stairs and shafts

Ceiling/floor voids, enclosed stairs, lift wells and riser-like spaces need careful zoning and clear text descriptions, especially in addressable systems.

06

WARNING DEVICES

Audible and visual alarm strategy

Warning devices must be clear, recognizable and suitable for the occupants and background noise of the building.

  • Public areas normally need a clear audible alarm level.
  • Sleeping areas require stronger audibility at the bedhead.
  • High-noise areas may require visual alarm devices such as strobes.
  • Fire alarm sound should be distinct from other warning systems.
  • Music or public entertainment sound may need automatic muting.
  • External indication may be useful for large buildings with multiple entrances.
07

DESIGN WORKFLOW

A practical workflow for fire alarm design

STEP 01

Agree the system category

Confirm whether the design is M, L1–L5, P1/P2 or a combined strategy.

STEP 02

Define zones and evacuation logic

Separate detection zones from alarm zones and document simple, phased or staged evacuation logic.

STEP 03

Select detectors by environment

Match detector type with fire risk, room use and false-alarm sources.

STEP 04

Place MCPs and warning devices

Locate manual call points, sounders and strobes so the system supports evacuation and is understandable on drawings.

STEP 05

Prepare cause-and-effect matrix

Document how detectors, MCPs and system inputs trigger alarms, relays, smoke control, elevators and supervisory actions.

STEP 06

Review installation, commissioning and maintenance

A good design must be installable, testable, maintainable and clear for handover documentation.

08

FINAL CHECKLIST

What must be checked before final submission

  • System category and design objective are clearly stated.
  • Detection zones and alarm zones are not confused.
  • Detector type matches room use and environmental conditions.
  • MCPs are coordinated with exits, stairs and escape routes.
  • Sounders/strobes are suitable for noise level and occupant needs.
  • Panel text, address labels and zone descriptions are clear.
  • False-alarm sources are considered.
  • Cause-and-effect logic is coordinated with other disciplines.
Important note

This page is an educational overview. Real projects must follow the applicable local code, approved fire strategy, AHJ requirements, product certifications and project-specific design documents.
