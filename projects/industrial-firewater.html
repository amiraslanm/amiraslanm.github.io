Fire Pump & Firewater Network Design for a Multi-Building Industrial Facility

Executive summary

Converting diverse demands into a coordinated design basis

The central challenge was coordinating the different demands of multiple industrial buildings through one centralized system. A defensible selection required defining the system boundary, checking input data, developing separate fire scenarios, and assessing flow, pressure, path loss, suction conditions and storage duration as an integrated design problem.

Engineering journey

Step-by-step engineering decision process

01

Define the system boundary

Buildings, consumers and centralized system boundaries were identified.

02

Verify input data

Elevations, routes, system types, line data and software outputs were reviewed.

03

Develop fire scenarios

Independent scenarios were defined to assess realistic simultaneous demands.

04

Review hydraulic calculations

Hazard classification, design area, elevation, path losses and calculation boundaries were checked.

05

Compare water and foam demands

Alternative scenarios were compared by flow, pressure and operating duration.

06

Identify the governing scenario

Selection was based on the combined flow and pressure requirement, not the largest isolated value.

07

Select and refine pump capacity

The initial selection was refined as inputs and concurrency assumptions were clarified, avoiding unnecessary oversizing.

08

Verify network component pressure

Operating pressure, churn pressure and network-component pressure capacity were considered.

09

Review suction and NPSH

Minimum water level, pump centreline, suction-line losses and NPSH margin were reviewed.

10

Size storage and document the design

Supply duration, usable volume, assumptions and required data were recorded in reports and technical correspondence.

Engineering judgement

Decisions that made the design defensible

01

Scenario separation

Realistic concurrency was assessed instead of indiscriminately summing all demands.

02

Oversizing control

Pump capacity was refined as data improved, without reducing safety.

03

Separate loss from pressure capacity

Hydraulic loss and component pressure capacity were treated as separate checks.

04

Full pump-curve review

Rated duty, increased flow, churn pressure and motor power require vendor confirmation.

05

Separate storage from suction

Storage determines duration; suction performance depends on level and line loss.

06

Document uncertainties

Missing data, assumptions and design limitations were documented.

Conclusion

The value was a traceable design logic

The main output was not a single value, but a documented process for converting project inputs into an engineering decision. This enabled scenario verification, input review, refinement of the initial selection and formal recording of limitations.
