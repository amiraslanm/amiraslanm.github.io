01

Fundamentals

Why does structural steel need fire protection?

Steel is noncombustible, but increasing temperature reduces its stiffness and load-bearing capacity. Fire protection slows the heating rate so the member can maintain structural stability for the required fire-resistance period.

R

Load-bearing capacity

The ability to maintain mechanical and structural function during fire.

E

Integrity

Prevention of flame and hot-gas passage through a separating element.

I

Insulation

Limiting temperature rise on the unexposed surface during testing.

Important distinction

Reaction to fire describes how a material contributes to ignition and flame spread; fire resistance describes how long a member or assembly maintains its required function during fire.

02

System selection

Common passive protection systems for steel

SFRM

Sprayed mineral coating

A thick, lightweight cementitious or gypsum-based layer that limits heat transfer. Adhesion, density and thickness control are critical.

INTUMESCENT

Intumescent coating

It expands under heat and forms an insulating char. It must not be directly covered by other materials and requires space to expand.

ENCLOSURE

Boards or protective enclosure

The member is enclosed by approved boards or a membrane assembly. Continuity, fixings, joints and details govern performance.

Selection principle

Coating type or thickness must not be selected from generic experience; it must come from the product technical assessment and design tables for the actual section, required fire-resistance period and service conditions.

01

Certificate and assessment report

Factory-produced steel fire protection must have a valid technical assessment appropriate to its type; Code 112 refers to EN 13381 and the legal technical-certification authority.

02

Complete member protection

Columns, primary beams and related members require continuous protection; column protection continues from structural floor to the structural soffit above and includes its connections.

03

No services embedded in protection

Pipes, wiring, ducts or building services must not be embedded within the protection required for an independently protected structural member.

04

Multidiscipline coordination

Suspended ceilings, MEP supports, structural connections, impact guards, anticorrosion paint and future maintenance must be coordinated before application.

Chemical incompatibility

Wet cementitious coatings create an alkaline environment. Alkali-sensitive anticorrosion paints, including some alkyd systems, can be damaged; mechanical mesh does not eliminate the need to verify chemical compatibility.

06

Quality control

Field inspection: adhesion, density and thickness

ASTM E736 / EN 1015-12

Adhesion testing

ASTM E736 commonly indicates internal cohesive strength, while EN 1015-12 with perimeter cutting can identify failure at the coating-substrate interface.

ASTM E605

Dry-density control

Samples are dried for at least 24 hours at 43±6°C; measured dry density should remain within ±15% of the design dry density.

ASTM E605

Thickness control

For each floor or approximately each 1000 m², at least one column, one primary beam and one secondary beam are checked, using two sections about 30 cm apart.

Thickness acceptance

The average thickness on each member must not be below design thickness. Local deficiency must also remain within the code limits of 6 mm and 25% of design thickness.

If a member fails, the deficient area is repaired and another randomly selected member of the same type is retested.

Source and scope
